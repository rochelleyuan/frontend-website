Blank

Introduction

Ever draw a blank when trying to decide what to do with a group of friends? Are you very indecisive? BLANK is a native mobile app inspired by this same problem, where groups of people have trouble finding an activity to do or what to eat together.

Tools

Sketch, InVision

Roles

UI Designer, UX Designer

Overview

In the app, users can create or join a room in which they’ll be able to match their chosen activity to that of other users’ in the same room. The result would be one chosen activity the group could do in their chosen location. This will reduce the amount of time spent on indecisively trying to find an activity.

Context

The goal of this project was to design the iOS and Android versions of a mobile app while following their respective design guidelines.

Objective

In light of the pandemic, the amount of activities people could have once done has reduced greatly. Many people already face indecisiveness, and with this new problem, it’s even harder to decide what activities to do. BLANK addresses this problem, even when social distancing is no longer necessary.

User Flow

To begin, I created a user flow to map out the basic structure of my app. I focused on the ability to create/join a room and matching.



Sketches

Then, I sketched out low-fidelity wireframes to match the user flows. I wanted to sketch mainly the function and how it was generally going to look, rather than first focusing on the different iOS/Android guidelines.

Competitor Analysis

I couldn’t find an app that had the same specific functions as my own, but I did take a look at other activity-finding mobile apps like Eventbrite and MeetUp. While looking at these apps, I analyzed the similarities and differences between their iOS and Android versions while also documenting what could work for my app and what wasn’t needed. After deciding what aspects I wanted to include in BLANK, I went ahead and added them to my digital wireframes.



Wireframes

After sketching the basic outline and looking at competitors, I headed over to Sketch to create the iOS and Android digital versions while following each of their respective design guidelines. I also added more screens since the flow from each screen in my sketches was too abrupt.

Illustrations

The colors were chosen in an effort to keep the app playful, since its function is more informal, while still appealing to the intended younger demographic. The font is more rounded to keep up the same vibe, but the illustrations help make the appearance more put together.



Prototype Feedback

With the fully fleshed out design, I prototyped it again in InVision and tested the design with 5 users (2 Android, and 3 iOS). I took notes on what went well and what didn’t go well. Some challenges I faced were seeing how the flow of me going through the app compared to someone new going through the app differed. Since I already knew how the app functioned, it was very easy for me to complete tasks while using the app. It was refreshing to see how certain tasks could be more confusing to a new user. Thus, with the notes, I highlighted what changes I needed to make that would help the flow and proceeded to edit my designs.

Takeaways

What Went Well

  • In the beginning, I didn’t thoroughly think through each user flow so I could have sketched more because I had to make up for it while I was going through the digital designs and feedback.

  • Although the iOS and Android screens differed a little, the overall appearance and feel of the app was still there.

  • Feedback was good and from each individual, I was able to get a feel of what they needed changed and implement it afterwards.

What Didn't Go Well

  • Initially, it was hard to compare my app with other apps because I didn’t find an app that works the same as mine. The ones that I did find felt were very different in scope.
